Defining Basic Elements & Six Standard Forms of Equations

Defining the Basic Elements

Al-Khwarizmi classified the components of an equation into three types:

  • “Roots” (shay’ in Arabic): The unknown quantity, corresponding to what we call x.
  • “Squares” (mal in Arabic): The square of the unknown (x2).
  • “Numbers”: Constant terms.

By consistently referring to these three components, he set a precedent for the symbolic structure of algebraic equations, though he himself mostly used rhetorical algebra (writing out phrases in words rather than symbols).

Six Standard Forms of Equations

Al-Khwarizmi categorized quadratic (and simpler) equations into six types. The idea was that any solvable equation could be reduced to one of these forms:

  • x2 = ax
  • x2 = b
  • x2 + ax = b
  • x2 + b = ax
  • ax + b = x2
  • x2 + ax + b = 0

In modern notation, some of these forms overlap (we often rewrite them in the single form x2 + px + q = 0), but Al-Khwarizmi’s systematic classification was revolutionary in standardization.
