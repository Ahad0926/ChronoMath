Introduction to Elementary Algebra and Quadratic Equations

In 820 CE, under the Abbasid Caliphate during the Islamic Golden Age, Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi (Al-Khwarizmi) published his treatise detailing various concepts in elementary algebra, known as al-Kitāb al-Mukhtaṣar fī Ḥisāb al-Jabr wal-Muqābalah (الكتاب المختصر في حساب الجبر والمقابلة), translated as The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing, more simply known as Al-Jabr (الجبر).

His primary focus was detailing how to deal with everyday issues regarding inheritance, commercial transactions, and the distribution of legacies, which often required calculations with unknown variables. As such, he codified a clear, step-by-step methodology for solving what is now known as linear and quadratic equations.

The treatise explained solving for positive roots of polynomial equations, from simpler linear equations up to the second degree. He also introduced “reducing” and “balancing” equations (the root meaning of “Al-Jabr”), moving subtracted terms from one side of an equation to the other— essentially what we now call canceling like terms on opposite sides.

Al-Jabr was the first text to teach algebra for its own sake and is the etymological source of the English word “algebra.” Its importance is profound for standardized algebra today, especially the application of solving for unknowns in polynomial equations.
