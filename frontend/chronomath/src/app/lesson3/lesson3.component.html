Methods of “Completion” & “Balancing,” Geometric Interpretations, and the Structure of Quadratic Equations

Methods of “Completion” (al-jabr) and “Balancing” (al-muqābala)

Completion (al-jabr): Involves adding equal terms to both sides of an equation to eliminate negative or fractional parts, thereby “completing” the square or the root. For instance, if the equation were x^2 + 10x = 39, completing the square might involve adding and subtracting 25.

Balancing (al-muqābala): Involves simplifying and reorganizing the equation by canceling out like terms on opposite sides. If an equation had x^2 + 10x + 21 on one side and 2x + 21 on the other, you would bring the terms together and “balance” them so all unknown terms appear on one side and constants on the other.

Geometric Interpretations

Al-Khwarizmi illustrated solutions to quadratic equations using geometric arguments. For instance, to solve x^2 + 10x = 39, he would represent x^2 as a square in a geometric diagram, then attach rectangular sections of area 10x and show how “completing the square” leads to a perfect square. These geometric interpretations lent intuitive and visual credibility to algebraic procedures.

The Structure of Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation typically has the form ax^2 + bx + c = 0, a ≠ 0. In many modern textbooks, these are simply referred to as the x^2-term, the x-term, and the constant.

Common Ways to Solve Quadratic Equations

Factoring: Look for two numbers that multiply to ac (if the equation is ax^2 + bx + c = 0) and add to b.

Completing the Square (Al-Khwarizmi's “Completion”):
1. Make the coefficient of x^2 equal to 1 (if a ≠ 1, divide the equation by a).
2. Rewrite x^2 + bx so that you can form a perfect square by adding (b/2)^2.
3. Balance the equation by adding this value to both sides.
4. Factor the left-hand side into a perfect square (x + b/2)^2.
5. Solve by taking square roots.

Quadratic Formula:
x = [-b ± √(b^2 - 4ac)] / (2a)

Graphical Interpretation:
Less explicitly used in Al-Khwarizmi's time but helpful today. The solutions (if real) are the x-intercepts of the parabola y = ax^2 + bx + c.
