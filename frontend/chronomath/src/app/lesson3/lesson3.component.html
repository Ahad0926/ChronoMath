Methods of “Completion” & “Balancing,” Geometric Interpretations, and the Structure of Quadratic Equations

Methods of “Completion” (al-jabr) and “Balancing” (al-muqābala)

Completion (al-jabr): Involves adding equal terms to both sides of an equation to eliminate negative or fractional parts, thereby “completing” the square or the root. For instance, if the equation were x2 + 10x = 39, completing the square might involve adding and subtracting 25.

Balancing (al-muqābala): Involves simplifying and reorganizing the equation by canceling out like terms on opposite sides. For example, if an equation had different terms on each side, you would bring the like terms together to “balance” the equation so that all unknowns appear on one side and the constants on the other.

Geometric Interpretations

Al-Khwarizmi illustrated solutions to quadratic equations using geometric arguments. For instance, to solve x2 + 10x = 39, he would represent x2 as a square in a geometric diagram, then attach rectangular sections of area 10x to show how “completing the square” leads to a perfect square. These geometric interpretations lent intuitive and visual credibility to algebraic procedures.

The Structure of Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation typically has the form ax2 + bx + c = 0, a ≠ 0. In many modern textbooks, these are simply referred to as the x2-term, the x-term, and the constant.

Common Ways to Solve Quadratic Equations

Factoring: Look for two numbers that multiply to ac (if the equation is ax2 + bx + c = 0) and add to b.

Completing the Square (Al-Khwarizmi's “Completion”):
1. Make the coefficient of x2 equal to 1 (if a ≠ 1, divide the equation by a).
2. Rewrite x2 + bx so that you can form a perfect square by adding (b/2)2.
3. Balance the equation by adding this value to both sides.
4. Factor the left-hand side into a perfect square (x + b/2)2.
5. Solve by taking square roots.
