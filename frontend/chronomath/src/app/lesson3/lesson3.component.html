Methods of “Completion” & “Balancing,” Geometric Interpretations, and the Structure of Quadratic Equations

Methods of “Completion” (al-jabr)

Completion (al-jabr) involves adding equal terms to both sides of an equation to eliminate negative or fractional parts, effectively “completing” the square or root. For instance, if x2 + 10x = 39, you might add and subtract 25 to form a perfect square.

Balancing (al-muqābala)

Balancing (al-muqābala) means simplifying and reorganizing the equation by canceling out like terms on opposite sides. For example, if an equation had different terms on each side, you’d bring the like terms together to “balance” it so that all unknowns appear on one side and the constants on the other.

Geometric Interpretations

Al-Khwarizmi illustrated solutions to quadratic equations using geometric arguments. For instance, to solve x2 + 10x = 39, he would represent x2 as a square, then attach rectangular sections of area 10x to show how “completing the square” leads to a perfect square. These geometric interpretations lent intuitive and visual credibility to algebraic procedures.

The Structure of Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation typically has the form ax2 + bx + c = 0, where a ≠ 0. In many modern textbooks, these are simply the x2-term, the x-term, and the constant.

Common Ways to Solve Quadratic Equations

Factoring: Look for two numbers that multiply to ac (if the equation is ax2 + bx + c = 0) and add to b.

Completing the Square (Al-Khwarizmi's “Completion”):
1. Make the coefficient of x2 = 1 (divide if needed).
2. Rewrite x2 + bx so you can form a perfect square by adding (b/2)2.
3. Balance the equation by adding this value to both sides.
4. Factor the left-hand side into a perfect square (x + b/2)2.
5. Solve by taking square roots.

Quadratic Formula: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / (2a)

Graphical Interpretation:
Less explicitly used in Al-Khwarizmi's time but helpful today, the solutions (if real) are the x-intercepts of the parabola described by y = ax2 + bx + c.
